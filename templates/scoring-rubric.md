# Scoring Rubric — 29 Dimensions in 5 Categories

Each dimension is scored on a 0-10 scale with 4 scoring bands. Dimensions are either **Core** (always scored) or **Contextual** (scored when applicable based on document type).

---

---

## Overall Score Calculation

Overall score = (sum of all scored dimension final scores / number of dimensions scored) × 10

This gives a 0-100 scale. The denominator varies per review based on document type. Always record how many dimensions were scored.

---

## Scoring Principles

1. **5 is average, not bad.** Most competent PMM work lands in the 5-7 range. Reserve 8+ for genuinely strong work and 9-10 for exceptional.
2. **Score the document, not the person.** Same rubric applies regardless of seniority.
3. **Both versions get scored.** The improvement delta is as important as the absolute score.
4. **Evidence is mandatory.** Every score must cite specific text from the document.
5. **Feedback must include rewrite examples.** Don't just say what's wrong — show what better looks like by rewriting the PMM's own text. Use the "Their line / Could become / Why this works" format. This is especially critical for Category B (Copywriting Craft) dimensions.
6. **Context matters.** A blog and a positioning doc have different expectations. Note when a dimension is less central.
7. **Score only applicable dimensions.** If a contextual dimension doesn't apply, skip it and note why. Don't force-fit.

# Category A: Strategic Foundation

These dimensions assess whether the content has a sound product marketing strategy — clear messaging, differentiated value, and a defined audience.

---

## A1. Messaging Clarity `[Core]`

How clear, concise, and consistent is the core message?

| Score | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| 0-3 | Vague, jargon-heavy, unclear who/what/why. Reader can't articulate the main point after reading. |
| 4-6 | Core message is present but buried, inconsistent across sections, or diluted by tangents. |
| 7-8 | Clear, concise, consistent messaging throughout. Reader immediately grasps the main point. |
| 9-10 | Exceptionally crisp. Passes the "elevator test" — anyone could repeat the message after one read. |

---

## A2. Value Proposition `[Core]`

How well does the content articulate differentiated value?

| Score | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| 0-3 | Features listed without benefit framing. No connection to customer problems or outcomes. |
| 4-6 | Benefits stated but generic ("save time", "increase efficiency") or unsupported by evidence. |
| 7-8 | Clear, differentiated value tied to specific customer pain points. Benefits are concrete and specific. |
| 9-10 | Compelling, unique, defensible value prop. Reader thinks "I can't get this anywhere else." |

---

## A3. Audience Targeting `[Core]`

How precisely is the content tailored to its intended audience?

| Score | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| 0-3 | No clear audience. Generic language that could apply to anyone. |
| 4-6 | Audience is implied but language isn't tailored. Uses insider jargon OR oversimplifies. |
| 7-8 | Specific persona clearly addressed. Language, examples, and pain points match the audience. |
| 9-10 | Deep audience insight. Speaks their exact language, references their world, anticipates objections. |

---

## A4. Competitive Positioning `[Contextual]`

How effectively does the content differentiate from alternatives?

| Score | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| 0-3 | No differentiation. Content could describe any competitor's product. |
| 4-6 | Some differentiation claimed but not defended with evidence or specifics. |
| 7-8 | Clear competitive frame with supporting evidence. Reader understands why this vs. alternatives. |
| 9-10 | Owns a unique position. Reframes the category or creates a frame competitors can't claim. |

---

## A5. CTA Effectiveness `[Contextual]`

How clear and compelling is the call to action?

| Score | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| 0-3 | No clear next step. Content ends passively or with a generic "learn more." |
| 4-6 | CTA exists but is generic, disconnected from the content, or buried. |
| 7-8 | Specific, motivating CTA that logically follows from the content. Reader knows exactly what to do. |
| 9-10 | Irresistible CTA. Creates urgency with integrity. The content builds inevitably toward this action. |

---

## A6. Evidence & Proof Points `[Core]`

How well does the content support claims with data, proof points, or examples?

| Score | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| 0-3 | Claims are unsupported. No data, customer quotes, examples, or proof points. |
| 4-6 | Some evidence present but generic ("thousands of customers"), dated, or poorly placed. |
| 7-8 | Relevant, specific data, customer quotes, or metrics placed where they strengthen the argument. |
| 9-10 | Compelling proof points that clinch the argument. Evidence is specific, credible, and persuasive. |

# Category B: Copywriting Craft

These dimensions assess the quality of the writing itself — independent of strategy. Is the copy tight, vivid, musical, and well-constructed? This is where craft lives. Reference: Gary Provost's "Write Music" principle — writing should have rhythm, variation, and musicality. Good copy is heard, not just read.

---

## B1. Headline & Hook Quality `[Contextual]`

How effectively does the opening grab attention and pull the reader in?

| Score | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| 0-3 | No clear headline or hook. Opens with boilerplate, company history, or a dictionary definition. |
| 4-6 | Headline exists but is generic or descriptive rather than compelling. Opening doesn't create pull. |
| 7-8 | Headline is specific and creates curiosity or urgency. Opening makes the reader want to continue. |
| 9-10 | Headline is impossible to scroll past. Opening creates an immediate "I need to keep reading" reaction. Uses a pattern interrupt, provocative question, or vivid scenario. |

---

## B2. Word Economy & Conciseness `[Core]`

How tight is the writing? Could the same point be made in fewer words?

| Score | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| 0-3 | Bloated. Full of filler phrases ("in order to", "it is important to note that"), redundancies, and padding. Key points buried in walls of text. |
| 4-6 | Some unnecessary words but generally readable. Occasional redundancy. Could be cut by 20-30%. |
| 7-8 | Tight writing. Every sentence earns its place. Minimal filler. Could cut maybe 5-10%. |
| 9-10 | Every word is load-bearing. Nothing could be cut without losing meaning. Writing has compression and density without sacrificing clarity. |

---

## B3. Specificity vs. Vagueness `[Core]`

Does the writing use concrete, specific language or fall back on abstract generalities?

| Score | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| 0-3 | Dominated by vague language: "best-in-class," "cutting-edge," "leverage," "robust solution." No concrete details. |
| 4-6 | Mix of specific and vague. Some concrete details but also falls back on marketing clichés and generic claims. |
| 7-8 | Predominantly specific. Numbers, names, timeframes, concrete examples. Vague language is rare. |
| 9-10 | Relentlessly specific. Every claim is grounded in something concrete and verifiable. Reader can visualize exactly what's being described. |

---

## B4. Sentence Rhythm & Musicality `[Core]`

*Reference: Gary Provost — "This sentence has five words. Here are five more words. Five-word sentences are fine. But several together become monotonous. Listen to what is happening. The writing is getting boring..."*

Does the writing have rhythm? Does it vary sentence length to create music? Short sentences punch. Long sentences carry the reader on a flowing, rhythmic journey that builds and builds before landing. The mix creates music.

| Score | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| 0-3 | Monotonous. Same sentence length and pattern throughout. Every sentence is 10-15 words, subject-verb-object. No variation. The writing is flat — it has no pulse. Reading it out loud reveals no music. |
| 4-6 | Some length variation but unintentional. Occasionally a short sentence appears, but the rhythm feels accidental, not crafted. No sense that the writer is hearing the words. |
| 7-8 | Deliberate rhythm. Short sentences used for emphasis and impact. Longer sentences used to build momentum or convey complexity. The writer clearly varies pace — fast when urgency matters, slower when nuance matters. Reading it aloud reveals a cadence. |
| 9-10 | The writing sings. Sentence length varies from 3 words to 30+ with purpose. Staccato bursts create tension. Flowing passages create momentum. One-word sentences land like a drumbeat. The rhythm reinforces the meaning — urgent ideas get short sentences, expansive ideas get room to breathe. You can hear this writing. |

---

## B5. Repetition & Parallel Structure `[Core]`

Is repetition used intentionally as a rhetorical device, or is it lazy and distracting? Are parallel constructions used to create power and memorability?

| Score | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| 0-3 | Unintentional repetition. Same words, phrases, or sentence openers repeated unconsciously ("We offer... We provide... We deliver..."). No parallel structure. Repetition feels like a limited vocabulary, not a choice. |
| 4-6 | Some unintentional repetition remains. Occasional parallel structure but inconsistent. The writer hasn't discovered repetition as a tool — it's an accident when it works. |
| 7-8 | Intentional use of repetition for emphasis. Anaphora (repeated openings), epistrophe (repeated endings), or tricolons (groups of three) appear where they amplify the message. Unintentional repetition is rare. Key phrases are echoed for reinforcement, not monotony. |
| 9-10 | Masterful rhetorical repetition. Parallel structures create memorable, quotable phrases. Callback loops connect the opening to the close. Key messages are repeated with variation — same idea, different angle — so the reader absorbs them without feeling beaten over the head. The repetition is invisible as technique but powerful in effect. |

---

## B6. Power Language & Verb Strength `[Core]`

Does the writing use strong, active verbs? Does it eliminate weak modifiers, passive voice, and hedge words?

| Score | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| 0-3 | Passive voice dominates ("is used by," "can be leveraged"). Weak verbs throughout ("is," "has," "does," "makes"). Hedge words everywhere ("somewhat," "relatively," "might help"). Nominalizations turn strong verbs into weak nouns ("implementation" instead of "implement," "utilization" instead of "use"). |
| 4-6 | Mix of active and passive. Some strong verbs but falls back on "is/has/does" frequently. Occasional hedge words. Writing has some energy but pulls its punches. |
| 7-8 | Predominantly active voice. Verbs carry weight — "slashes," "eliminates," "transforms" rather than "helps reduce," "enables improvement." Hedge words are rare and intentional. Writing has confidence in its verb choices. |
| 9-10 | Verbs do the heavy lifting. Every sentence is driven by a specific, muscular verb. No unnecessary modifiers — the verb says it all. Active voice throughout. The writing doesn't describe action — it is action. Zero nominalizations. Zero "very/really/extremely." |

---

## B7. Sensory & Vivid Language `[Contextual]`

Does the writing paint pictures? Does it use concrete imagery, analogies, and metaphors that make abstract concepts tangible?

| Score | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| 0-3 | Entirely abstract. No imagery, no analogies, no sensory details. The writing lives in the world of concepts and never touches the ground. Reader can't visualize anything. |
| 4-6 | Occasional concrete image but mostly abstract. Analogies attempted but clichéd ("like a Swiss Army knife") or mixed. Imagery is decoration, not load-bearing. |
| 7-8 | Effective use of vivid language. Analogies are fresh and clarifying — they make complex ideas click. Concrete details anchor abstract concepts. Reader can picture what's being described. |
| 9-10 | Writing creates mental movies. Analogies are original and perfectly matched to the audience's experience. Abstract concepts are made tangible through imagery that sticks. The reader remembers the image long after the details fade. "Show, don't tell" is second nature. |

---

## B8. Emotional Resonance & Empathy `[Contextual]`

Does the writing connect emotionally with the reader? Does it demonstrate understanding of their situation?

| Score | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| 0-3 | No emotional connection. Reads like a spec sheet. No acknowledgment of the reader's challenges, frustrations, or aspirations. |
| 4-6 | Some attempt at empathy but feels performative or generic ("we understand your challenges"). Doesn't ring true. |
| 7-8 | Genuine empathy. Names specific frustrations the reader recognizes. Creates a "they get me" moment. Balances emotion with substance. |
| 9-10 | Deep emotional resonance. Reader feels seen and understood. Pain points are articulated more precisely than the reader could express themselves. Aspirational elements inspire without being empty. |

---

## B9. Opening & Closing Craft `[Contextual]`

Does the opening create a loop the reader needs to close? Does the ending land with impact and bring the piece full circle?

| Score | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| 0-3 | Opening is throat-clearing ("In today's fast-paced world..."). Closing trails off, restates the obvious, or abruptly stops. No relationship between how the piece opens and closes. |
| 4-6 | Opening gets to the point but doesn't create pull. Closing summarizes but doesn't resonate. The piece starts and stops rather than opens and lands. |
| 7-8 | Strong opening that creates a question, tension, or curiosity the reader wants resolved. Closing delivers on the promise of the opening. Reader feels the piece is complete — nothing is missing, nothing is wasted. |
| 9-10 | Opening is a hook that won't let go — it creates a narrative tension or poses a question that compels the reader forward. Closing is a callback that reframes the opening with new meaning. The piece feels circular and inevitable. The last line lingers. |

---

## B10. Readability & Accessibility `[Core]`

Is the writing easy to read for the intended audience? Is the reading level appropriate?

| Score | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| 0-3 | Dense, academic, or impenetrable. Long paragraphs with no breaks. Assumes knowledge the audience may not have. Reading feels like work. |
| 4-6 | Readable but uneven. Some sections flow, others require re-reading. Paragraph length varies wildly. |
| 7-8 | Consistently easy to read. Appropriate paragraph length. Complex ideas broken into digestible pieces. Reading feels effortless. |
| 9-10 | Exceptionally accessible without being dumbed down. Reader absorbs complex information without realizing the complexity. Writing disappears — only the ideas remain. |

# Category C: Structure & Logic

These dimensions assess how well the content is organized — does it build a logical argument, guide the reader, and make information easy to find?

---

## C1. Information Hierarchy `[Core]`

Is the most important information presented first? Is the structure organized by importance, not chronology or convenience?

| Score | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| 0-3 | No clear hierarchy. Important points buried. Structured by how the author thought of things, not by what the reader needs. |
| 4-6 | Some hierarchy but key points aren't in the strongest positions (opening, headings, first sentences of paragraphs). |
| 7-8 | Clear hierarchy. Most important information leads. Headings and topic sentences signal priority. Reader can skim and get the main points. |
| 9-10 | Inverted-pyramid structure throughout. Every section opens with its key takeaway. A reader who stops at any point has already absorbed the most important content up to that point. |

---

## C2. Argument Structure `[Core]`

Does the content build a logical argument? Is there a clear claim → evidence → conclusion flow?

| Score | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| 0-3 | No discernible argument. Assertions are made without support. Sections feel like a random collection of facts. |
| 4-6 | An argument exists but has logical gaps. Claims sometimes precede context. Conclusions don't always follow from evidence. |
| 7-8 | Clear argument structure. Problem → solution → evidence → outcome flow. Each section builds the case logically. |
| 9-10 | Airtight logic. Every claim is set up, supported, and connected to the next. Counter-arguments are anticipated. The conclusion feels inevitable, not asserted. |

---

## C3. Transition Quality `[Core]`

How well does the content flow between sections and ideas? Do transitions guide the reader?

| Score | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| 0-3 | No transitions. Sections are disconnected blocks. Reader has to figure out how ideas relate. Abrupt topic changes. |
| 4-6 | Basic transitions exist ("additionally," "furthermore") but feel mechanical. Some gaps between ideas. |
| 7-8 | Smooth transitions that show the relationship between ideas. Reader never feels lost or jarred by a topic change. |
| 9-10 | Seamless flow. Each section's ending creates natural momentum into the next. The reader is carried forward without noticing the transitions — they feel inevitable. |

---

## C4. Scannability & Formatting `[Contextual]`

Can the reader quickly find what they need? Are headers, bullets, bold text, and whitespace used effectively?

| Score | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| 0-3 | Wall of text. No headers, bullets, or formatting aids. Reader must read everything linearly to find anything. |
| 4-6 | Some formatting but inconsistent. Headers exist but aren't descriptive. Bullets used but not strategically. |
| 7-8 | Well-formatted. Descriptive headers, strategic use of bold, consistent bullet formatting. Reader can scan and find key information in seconds. |
| 9-10 | Optimally scannable. Headers tell a story on their own. Bold text highlights exactly the right phrases. White space creates breathing room. The document works both for scanners and deep readers. |

# Category D: Persuasion & Conversion

These dimensions assess how effectively the content moves the reader toward belief and action.

---

## D1. Social Proof Placement `[Contextual]`

Are customer stories, testimonials, logos, and social validation used effectively?

| Score | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| 0-3 | No social proof. All claims are self-reported. No external validation of any kind. |
| 4-6 | Some social proof but generic ("trusted by leading companies") or poorly placed (all lumped at the end). |
| 7-8 | Specific, credible social proof placed where it reinforces key claims. Named customers with real metrics. |
| 9-10 | Social proof is woven throughout, appearing exactly where the reader might be skeptical. Each proof point is specific, relevant to the audience, and placed for maximum persuasive impact. |

---

## D2. Benefit vs. Feature Ratio `[Core]`

Does the content lead with benefits (outcomes for the reader) or features (product capabilities)?

| Score | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| 0-3 | Feature dump. Lists capabilities with no connection to customer outcomes. Reads like a spec sheet. |
| 4-6 | Mix of features and benefits, but features dominate. Benefits are generic when present ("save time"). |
| 7-8 | Benefits lead. Features are introduced as proof of how the benefit is delivered. Reader understands what they'll achieve, not just what the product does. |
| 9-10 | Benefit-first throughout. Every feature is framed through the lens of a customer outcome. Reader can immediately translate every capability into impact on their work. |

---

## D3. Objection Handling `[Contextual]`

Does the content anticipate and address likely reader objections or concerns?

| Score | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| 0-3 | No awareness of potential objections. Assumes the reader is already convinced. Ignores obvious concerns (price, migration, risk). |
| 4-6 | Some objections acknowledged but dismissed rather than addressed ("don't worry about X"). Feels defensive. |
| 7-8 | Key objections anticipated and addressed with evidence. Reframes concerns as advantages. Reader's doubts are reduced. |
| 9-10 | Masterful objection handling. Brings up concerns before the reader thinks of them, then resolves each with specific evidence. Turns weaknesses into strengths. Reader feels their concerns were heard and answered. |

---

## D4. Urgency & Trust Signals `[Contextual]`

Does the content create appropriate urgency and build trust? (Without being manipulative.)

| Score | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| 0-3 | No urgency or trust signals. Content is passive. Or: uses manipulative tactics (fake scarcity, countdown timers with no real deadline). |
| 4-6 | Some urgency attempted but generic ("don't miss out") or trust signals are superficial (badge walls, unverifiable claims). |
| 7-8 | Appropriate urgency tied to real costs of inaction. Trust signals are specific and verifiable (uptime SLA, specific guarantees, named references). |
| 9-10 | Creates genuine FOMO by articulating the cost of the status quo with specificity. Trust signals are deeply credible and placed exactly where the reader needs reassurance. Urgency feels helpful, not pushy. |

---

## D5. Technical Simplification `[Contextual]`

How well does the content make complex or technical ideas accessible to the target audience?

| Score | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| 0-3 | Unexplained jargon. Assumes deep technical expertise. Non-experts are lost. |
| 4-6 | Some simplification attempted but inconsistent. Some terms explained, others assumed. |
| 7-8 | Technical concepts made accessible without dumbing down. Analogies and examples bridge knowledge gaps. Appropriate for the target audience. |
| 9-10 | Complex ideas explained so clearly that a non-expert fully understands. Simplification feels effortless. Technical readers don't feel condescended to. |

# Category E: Brand & Voice

These dimensions assess whether the writing has a distinctive, consistent, and appropriately calibrated voice.

---

## E1. Tone Consistency `[Core]`

Is the tone consistent throughout the document? Does it maintain the same register from start to finish?

| Score | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| 0-3 | Tone shifts unpredictably. Formal in one paragraph, casual in the next. Hedging in the intro, aggressive in the close. Feels like multiple authors. |
| 4-6 | Generally consistent but with occasional lapses. A casual tone slips into corporate speak in places. |
| 7-8 | Consistent tone throughout. The same voice is maintained from opening to close. Register is appropriate for the audience and doc type. |
| 9-10 | Rock-solid consistency. Every sentence feels like it comes from the same voice. Tone shifts are intentional and purposeful (e.g., more serious for a proof point, lighter for a CTA). |

---

## E2. Brand Voice Distinctiveness `[Core]`

Does the writing sound like this specific brand, or could it be anyone's content?

| Score | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| 0-3 | Completely generic. Could swap in any competitor's name and it would still work. No personality. |
| 4-6 | Serviceable but undifferentiated. Professional and clear, but forgettable. Could be any B2B company. |
| 7-8 | Distinctive voice that reflects the brand's personality. Has identifiable characteristics (e.g., direct and punchy, or warm and expert, or irreverent and bold). |
| 9-10 | Unmistakably this brand. Reader could identify the company from the writing alone. Voice reinforces brand positioning and is consistent with other brand touchpoints. |

---

## E3. Confidence Calibration `[Core]`

Is the confidence level appropriate? Neither over-claiming nor hedge-heavy?

| Score | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| 0-3 | Severely miscalibrated. Either wildly overclaims ("the only solution you'll ever need") or hedges everything ("we think this might possibly help some companies potentially"). |
| 4-6 | Mostly appropriate but with occasional overclaims or unnecessary hedging. "Industry-leading" without evidence, or "we believe" before a factual statement. |
| 7-8 | Well-calibrated. Confident where evidence supports it. Appropriately measured where claims are softer. No empty superlatives. |
| 9-10 | Perfectly calibrated. Confidence level matches evidence level for every claim. Strong statements are backed by strong evidence. Uncertain areas are framed as opportunities, not weaknesses. Reader trusts the author because nothing feels inflated or deflated. |

---

## E4. Jargon Appropriateness `[Contextual]`

Is the level of specialized language appropriate for the target audience?

| Score | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| 0-3 | Jargon mismatch. Either drowns in undefined technical terms (for a non-technical audience) or oversimplifies condescendingly (for an expert audience). |
| 4-6 | Mostly appropriate but inconsistent. Some terms assumed, others over-explained. Doesn't fully commit to a vocabulary level. |
| 7-8 | Jargon level matches audience expertise. Technical terms used where the audience expects them, simplified where they don't. New terms introduced with context. |
| 9-10 | Perfect vocabulary calibration. Uses the audience's own language — the terms they use in meetings, Slack, and job descriptions. Reader never stumbles on a word or feels talked down to. |
