# Build with Claude Code for PMMs #2: The PMM Improvement Model

**A framework for measuring product marketing performance — because "my manager says my messaging has gotten better" isn't a growth story.**

---

## The question nobody can answer

It's appraisal season. Your manager asks: "How have you grown this year?"

You pull together a list. Five launches. Three competitive positioning docs. A messaging overhaul. Two case studies. You know you're better than you were twelve months ago. You can feel it in the work.

But you can't prove it.

You can point to LP conversion rates. Maybe campaign metrics. But that measures how the market responded to the work — not the quality of the craft itself. Did your messaging get sharper? Did your positioning become more differentiated? Are your first drafts closer to final quality, or are you still relying on three rounds of editing to get there?

You don't know. Your manager doesn't know either. They have a gut feeling. You have a gut feeling. Both of you walk into the review with a collection of anecdotes and hope the story holds together.

Meanwhile, your sales counterpart pulls up their Gong dashboard. Win rate: 34%, up from 28%. Discovery score: 8.2. Deal cycle: down 12 days. Negotiation: still a growth area. Clear. Specific. Trackable.

PMMs don't have an equivalent. Not a tool gap — a process gap. Nobody has defined what PMM performance actually consists of at a dimension level, so there's nothing to measure, nothing to track, and nothing to point to when the promotion conversation comes around.

## Why this gap hits harder than it looks

Here's what I think most people miss about this problem.

When a manager reviews your messaging doc and says "this needs to be tighter" — what does that actually mean? Are the sentences too long? Is the value prop buried? Is the audience targeting off? Is the tone inconsistent? "Tighter" could be fifteen different things. You pick the interpretation that makes sense to you, revise accordingly, and hope you guessed right.

Sometimes you did. Sometimes you didn't, and the same note comes back next time, phrased slightly differently.

This is the daily experience of being a PMM. You're not a copywriter, not a strategist, not a brand manager — you're all of those, receiving feedback that's well-intentioned but imprecise. And the only hard metric anyone can point to is LP conversion, which says nothing about whether your writing got better.

The result isn't just fuzzy appraisals. It's a coaching problem. Managers give feedback doc by doc, but they can't track whether the same patterns keep appearing. Was this the third time they've told you to lead with benefits instead of features? Neither of you remembers. So the note gets given again. Both sides get frustrated without knowing why.

I wanted to build a framework that names these things. What does PMM craft actually consist of? What are the dimensions? How do you track them?

## What the framework looks like

I landed on 29 dimensions across five categories. Each one isolates a specific skill that shows up in PMM work — and that can be coached independently.

**A. Strategic Foundation** — the "what are you saying" layer. Is the core message clear and repeatable? Is the value prop differentiated, or could you swap in any competitor's name? Is the audience actually targeted — specific persona, specific pain — or is this written for "companies"? Are claims backed by evidence, or is it assertion after assertion?

*Six dimensions: messaging clarity, value proposition, audience targeting, evidence & proof points (scored on every doc) + competitive positioning, CTA effectiveness (scored when the doc type calls for them).*

This is where most early-career PMMs struggle, and it's where most feedback should start. A beautifully written doc with the wrong message is still wrong.

**B. Copywriting Craft** — the sentence-level layer. Are 20 words doing the job of 10? Are the verbs carrying weight ("reduces pipeline incidents by 73%") or sitting there passively ("helps improve operations")? Does the rhythm vary — short punches, then a longer sentence that builds momentum — or is every line the same 15-word march?

*Ten dimensions — the mechanics: word economy, specificity, power language, readability (scored on every doc) + headline & hook quality (contextual). The music: sentence rhythm, repetition & parallel structure (scored on every doc) + vivid language, emotional resonance, opening & closing craft (contextual).*

This category is inspired by Gary Provost's "Write Music" principle. Read your doc out loud. If it sounds like a robot wrote it, the rhythm is dead. Most PMM writing dies here. Not because the strategy is wrong, but because every sentence has the same cadence, the same structure, the same flat energy.

**C. Structure & Logic** — the architecture. Most important information first? Argument flowing from claim to evidence to conclusion? Headers that tell a story on their own, so a skim gives you the point?

*Four dimensions: information hierarchy, argument structure, transitions (scored on every doc) + scannability & formatting (contextual).*

**D. Persuasion & Conversion** — leading with outcomes or features? Objections anticipated or ignored? Social proof placed next to the claim it supports, or buried in a section three scrolls down?

*Five dimensions: benefit vs. feature ratio (scored on every doc) + social proof placement, objection handling, urgency & trust signals, technical simplification (contextual — scored when the doc type calls for them).*

**E. Brand & Voice** — does this sound like your brand, or could it be anyone's B2B doc? Is the confidence calibrated — strong where you have evidence, measured where you don't — or is everything either overclaiming or hedge-heavy?

*Four dimensions: tone consistency, brand voice distinctiveness, confidence calibration (scored on every doc) + jargon appropriateness (contextual).*

17 of these dimensions are core — scored on every document. 12 are contextual — scored only when they apply to the doc type. A messaging doc gets competitive positioning and scannability. A blog post gets headline quality and emotional resonance. A landing page gets nearly everything.

Each dimension uses a 0–10 scale with four defined bands: Needs Work (0–3), Developing (4–6), Proficient (7–8), Exceptional (9–10). Clear rubrics. When your manager says "you're a 5 on specificity," both of you know exactly what that means — and what a 7 looks like.

This is the shared language that's been missing. Sales had MEDDIC before they had Gong. PMM has never had the equivalent. Now it does.

## What makes this usable: the Focus 3

Here's the design decision I care about most, because it came from watching what actually happens when you hand someone a page of feedback.

They shut down. They fix the easiest thing, not the most impactful. They ignore the rest. Next review, same notes. The manager wonders if the PMM isn't trying. The PMM wonders if they'll ever be good enough. Both sides are wrong, and neither side knows it.

Twenty-nine scores is data. It's not coaching.

So the framework surfaces exactly three priorities per review. Two quick wins — things you can fix in the current draft, in 15–30 minutes. One deeper lift — something that requires stepping back and rethinking a section. That's it. Everything else is explicitly "Parked for Later," with a reason.

The prioritization follows a hierarchy. Strategy before craft — always. If your value proposition isn't landing, no amount of verb optimization matters. High impact, low effort first — because momentum matters more than perfection. If the PMM has active improvement goals, at least one Focus 3 item aligns with them — because coaching should be coherent across reviews, not random.

And every piece of feedback includes a rewrite using the PMM's own text. Not "improve specificity." Not "add more evidence." Instead:

```
Their line:  "The visual pipeline builder lets analysts create their
              own data flows without filing an engineering ticket."
Could become: "Analysts build their own pipelines — no SQL, no ticket.
               Vanta's data team went from 8-hour turnaround to 30 min."
Why it works: Moves a proof point next to the claim it supports.
              Evidence beside the claim is more persuasive than evidence
              three sections later.
```

The PMM doesn't have to imagine what "better" looks like. They can see it. In their own words, applied to their own doc. The gap between where they are and where they could be becomes concrete, not abstract.

"Parked for Later" is just as important. It tells the PMM: these dimensions need work too, but not now. Don't worry about sentence rhythm while your value prop is still landing. That permission — the explicit "not yet" — is what keeps the process from becoming overwhelming. Three things. Start with the first one. It'll take 15 minutes.

The distance between "write better" and "your power language score is 5.5 — here are three verbs in your value prop that could be stronger, and here's what they'd look like" is the distance between being stuck and making progress.

## The metrics that actually answer "am I getting better?"

Two things the framework tracks that most feedback processes completely miss.

**Feedback retention: did coaching actually land?**

When a PMM submits iteration 2 of a campaign (Draft 2 → Draft 3), the framework reads every feedback item from the prior review and checks: addressed? Partially? Ignored? With quoted evidence from the new draft.

This is the signal managers need. An 80% retention rate means feedback is landing. A 30% rate means something's off. And here's the part I'm most deliberate about: **the framework doesn't assume the PMM is the problem.** If the same note appears three times without improvement, it flags the coaching method, not the person. Maybe written feedback isn't working. Maybe they need to see examples. Maybe a 20-minute pairing session would do what five rounds of comments couldn't.

"The PMM isn't trying" becomes "the approach isn't working." That reframe changes the coaching relationship entirely.

**Draft quality progression: the metric that separates growth from editing.**

This is the one that matters for promotions. Forget final scores — those reflect three rounds of revision and manager input. The real question is about the initial drafts.

```
Campaign A (messaging doc)     Draft 1 avg: 3.2
Campaign B (launch brief)      Draft 1 avg: 4.8
Campaign C (blog post)         Draft 1 avg: 5.9
```

If Draft 1 quality is climbing, the PMM is internalizing feedback. They're not just fixing what you told them — they're arriving at the next project with sharper instincts. Their first drafts are starting to look like what their third drafts used to look like. That's growth. That's the evidence that makes a promotion case concrete.

If Draft 1 quality stays flat across campaigns, the PMM is responsive but not learning. They'll always need the same depth of editing. That's not a failure — it's a data point that changes the coaching conversation. Maybe the improvement track needs different goals. Maybe they need exposure to stronger examples, not more notes on their own work.

Either way, you're having the conversation with evidence. Not vibes.

**The reports that make it actionable**

The framework generates five report types, each built for a specific conversation:

A **promotion report** pulls growth trajectory, draft quality progression, patterns that shifted from weakness to strength, and feedback retention rates — the entire case in one document. A **learning report** focuses exclusively on whether the PMM is internalizing feedback: Draft 1 scores across campaigns over time, persistent gaps, dimensions where initial quality improved vs. dimensions where they're still relying on editing. A **team report** builds the dimension heatmap across all PMMs — team-wide gaps become training needs, standouts become peer mentors. And a **comparison report** puts two PMMs side by side on the same dimensions.

The promotion report is the one that changes appraisal conversations. "Her initial draft quality improved from 3.2 to 5.9 over six months. She acts on 85% of coaching notes. Specificity and evidence moved from weaknesses to strengths." That's not a feeling about someone's trajectory. That's evidence your VP can read in 30 seconds.

A PMM can also run this solo. Submit your own Draft 1 and Draft 2 through the framework before your manager sees it. Track your own patterns. Build your own promotion case from the data. The growth system doesn't have to belong to your manager — it can belong to you.

## Making the growth story visible

Everything above generates evidence. But evidence locked in markdown files is evidence nobody sees. Your VP isn't going to read a 130-line profile with running averages by category. Your PMM isn't going to parse dimension trajectory tables. The data needs a visual layer — not because it's prettier, but because three distinct conversations need visual support, and each one needs different data at a different altitude.

I built a dashboard with three views.

**The team view: "Who needs attention?"**

This is the manager's morning scan. Cards for each PMM with a score ring showing their latest overall score and bars for each skill category. A recent activity feed shows which reviews were run and when.

You're not reading dimension scores here. You're scanning. Which rings are green and which are amber? Whose Copywriting Craft bar is noticeably shorter than their Strategic Foundation? Who hasn't had a review in six weeks?

The team view answers the question every manager carries around but rarely has data for: where should I spend my coaching time this week? Not on the PMM who's consistently green across the board. On the one whose strategy scores are climbing but whose craft scores haven't moved in two campaigns. That's where your time actually moves the needle.

**The skills heatmap: "Where are the real gaps?"**

This is the view that changes team-level decisions. All your PMMs down one side, all 29 skills across the top, each cell color-coded by final score — dark green for strong, amber for developing, red for needs work. Team averages in the rightmost column.

The first time you look at this, something jumps out that you couldn't see in individual reviews: patterns that span the team. Three out of four PMMs scoring below 5 on objection handling. Everyone strong on messaging clarity but weak on sentence rhythm. One PMM scoring 9 on competitive positioning while everyone else sits at 5.

These stop being individual coaching notes. A team-wide gap in objection handling is a workshop. A standout in competitive positioning is a peer mentor you didn't know you had. Without the heatmap, you'd discover these patterns across dozens of reviews over months. With it, five seconds.

**The individual view: "What's my shape?"**

This is where the PMM sees themselves. For a lot of people, it's the first time they've ever had a visual picture of their craft profile.

The centerpiece is a radar chart — a pentagon with one axis per skill category, showing two overlapping shapes. The dashed shape is their initial draft. The solid shape is their final version. The gap between the two is how much the editing process is doing for them.

A PMM whose two shapes nearly overlap has sharp instincts — first drafts already close to final quality. A big gap means responsive to feedback but not yet internalizing it. Neither is a judgment — it's a starting point.

Below the radar: top 5 strongest skills, top 5 growth opportunities, biggest improvements from draft to final, project history, and current Focus 3 priorities. When a PMM can see that their Strategic Foundation pentagon is wide but their Copywriting Craft axis is stunted, they don't need someone to tell them where to focus. They can see it. And next quarter, they can see whether the shape changed.

**What changes when the data is visible**

The VP stops asking "how's the team doing?" as a pleasantry and starts asking "why is everyone low on objection handling?" — a better question that leads somewhere. The PMM stops thinking of feedback as a checklist and starts watching their own shape evolve. The manager sees that sentence rhythm has been flagged for three campaigns without moving and thinks "maybe I need to show examples instead of writing notes." Visibility doesn't just present data — it changes which questions people ask.

## How I built it

Same philosophy as article #1: Claude Code, markdown files, no dependencies.

The framework is structured markdown — scoring rubrics, review templates, profile templates. The dashboard is a Python script (stdlib only) that parses every markdown file and generates a single self-contained HTML. All CSS, JavaScript, chart rendering — inline SVG, no charting library. No server, no CDN calls. Generate and open.

One design decision: the framework uses "dimensions," "campaigns," "iterations." Right for a scoring rubric. Wrong for a dashboard a VP glances at. So the dashboard translates. "Dimensions" become "skills." "Campaigns" become "projects." Small shifts that make the tool feel built for the user, not the designer.

The hardest part was the Focus 3 prioritization logic. What if the PMM has improvement goals — do those get weighted? What if it's iteration 3 and the Focus 3 from iteration 1 was never addressed — do you repeat it or move on? (You don't move on. That teaches people feedback is optional.)

## What's next: from document craft to competency

The current system measures what shows up in documents — 29 dimensions of craft you can see on the page. But PMM performance is bigger than document quality.

Sales enablement effectiveness. Stakeholder management. People management for senior PMMs. These are the competency layers that separate a strong writer from a strong PMM leader — and none of them live in a messaging doc. A Slack message from a sales rep thanking you for the battlecard that closed a deal, a manager's written account of how you handled a cross-functional launch — that's the evidence. The next version of this system needs to accept it.
